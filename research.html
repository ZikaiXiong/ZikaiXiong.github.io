Interior-point Methods Strike Back: Solving the Wasserstein Barycenter Problem (Accepted by NeurIPS 2019)

Primary Contributor, Advised by Prof. Dongdong Ge(SUFE) and Prof. Yinyu Ye(Stanford)

Computing the Wasserstein barycenter of a set of probability measures under the optimal transport metric can quickly become prohibitive for traditional second-order algorithms, such as interior-point methods, as the support size of the measures increases. In this paper, we overcome the difficulty by developing a new adapted interior-point method that fully exploits the problem's special matrix structure to reduce the iteration complexity and speed up the Newton procedure. Different from regularization approaches, our method achieves a well-balanced tradeoff between accuracy and speed. A numerical comparison on various distributions with existing algorithms exhibits the computational advantages of our approach. Moreover, we demonstrate the practicality of our algorithm on image benchmark problems including MNIST and Fashion-MNIST.

Effect of Hidden-city Ticketing in Revenue Management (manuscript)

Primary Contributor, Advised by Prof. Bo Jiang(SUFE)

Hidden-city ticketing is an interesting phenomenon in airline pricing. It refers to the case that a flight with a layover is cheaper than the flight from the origin to that intermediate city. When it happens, passengers may have the incentive to purchase the longer but less expensive flight, only take the portion from the origin to the layover and leave the flight legs after the intermediate city unused. In this paper, we establish a dynamic programming network pricing model to obtain some quantitative bounds of the revenue when the hidden-city tickets are both well-observed and partially-observed. Different from former literature, we analyze the effect from parallel flights of one O-D pair, and the choice probability of a specific product in our dynamic programming model is determined by some discrete choice models, including multinomial logit (MNL) choice model. In the worst case that all the passengers are aware of the hidden-city opportunities, we prove that the airlines should take reactions to eliminate all those hidden-city tickets. Nevertheless, even if the airlines choose the optimal strategy, the revenue is still less than what they could have earned in the ideal case that passengers are strictly forbidden to practice hidden-city ticketing. Moreover, we prove that if the flight network has a spoke-and-hub network structure and the airlines can take optimal pricing strategies, the decrease in revenue is no more than half of the ideal case. This quantitative bound is also demonstrated optimal in experiments. In the case that only a small portion of passengers can observe and practice hidden-city opportunities, we show that if the airlines take the optimal pricing strategies, the revenue could even be higher than the ideal case. Moreover, compared with the ideal case, we also prove that sometimes the decrease of the revenue can increase super linearly with respect to the proportion of the passengers that can practice hidden-city ticketing. Results of experiments match the theory as well.(The extended abstract will be updated in Janurary.)
